# Editorial Principles

## The editorial promise

Every asset should make the reader smarter.

## Principles

### 1. Start with the reader's problem
Do not start with the company or the product. Start with what the reader is trying to do and why it is hard.

### 2. Teach the category
Great content should help the market understand what matters, not just what the product does.

### 3. Be precise about technical concepts
Do not blur together evals, observability, monitoring, tracing, testing, and governance. These are different things with different uses.

### 4. Show the workflow
Explain what someone would actually do, in what order, and with what tools. Abstract advice is less useful than concrete steps.

### 5. Name failure modes
Failure modes are often more useful than benefits. Teams learn more from "here is how this breaks" than "here is how great this is."

### 6. Connect product to practice
Do not bolt the product onto the end of an educational post. Show how product capabilities support the workflow throughout.

### 7. Do not overclaim
Credibility compounds over time. One unsupported claim can undermine an otherwise excellent piece.

### 8. Edit aggressively
Cut anything generic. If a sentence could appear in any competitor's content, it should not appear here.

### 9. Build reusable ideas
Prefer frameworks, series, and repeatable patterns over one-off posts.

### 10. Make distribution part of strategy
A content asset is not done until it has a channel plan. Write with distribution in mind from the start.
